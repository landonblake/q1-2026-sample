§ I The Big Picture
A Comprehensive Narrative of the First Quarter

The first ten weeks of 2026 told a story that Washington has been rehearsing for years, now finally played out in full view: a Congress fractured along the fault lines of immigration policy, a national debt accelerating toward $39 trillion, a lobbying machine running at record pace, and — remarkably — a rare burst of actual legislative productivity. What began as an appropriations standoff left over from the longest government shutdown in American history ended with eleven of twelve spending bills signed into law. The twelfth remains a live grenade.

To understand Q1 2026, you have to understand what came before it. In the fall of 2025, the United States endured an extended government shutdown that paralyzed large swaths of federal operations. When lawmakers finally reached a deal in November 2025, they funded three agencies in full and kept the remaining nine on a temporary continuing resolution (CR) through January 30, 2026. The clock was ticking as the new year began.

January was dominated by the appropriations sprint. By January 22, the House had assembled and passed the final six of the year's twelve spending bills into a single package. Then, four days later, the calculus shifted. On January 24, Customs and Border Protection agents killed two American citizens — Alex Pretti and Renee Nicole Good — during protests in Minneapolis. The shooting ignited a political firestorm that would consume the next six weeks of congressional activity.

"People are tired of the uncertainty. It causes so much disruption." — Johnny Jones, Secretary/Treasurer, AFGE TSA Council 100, on the ongoing DHS shutdown

Senate Democrats, citing the Minneapolis shootings, refused to support the Department of Homeland Security funding bill without legislative reforms to DHS immigration operations — body cameras for ICE agents, tighter warrant standards, revised use-of-force rules. Senate Republicans resisted nearly every proposed restriction. After a tense week of negotiations, a compromise emerged on January 29: pass five of the six remaining bills immediately, fund DHS on a two-week CR, and continue negotiating. The Senate passed this framework 71–29. But the House dragged its feet, and funding lapsed anyway over the weekend of January 31–February 2 — the first shutdown of 2026, affecting roughly half the federal government.

The House ultimately resolved the brief impasse, passing the package 217–214 on February 3. President Trump signed it the same day as Public Law 119-75. For a few weeks, it appeared the worst was over. Eleven of twelve agencies were now funded through September 30, 2026, and the broader federal enterprise — Defense, Labor, HHS, Education, State, Transportation, HUD — had real, full-year money for the first time in nearly two years.

⚠ Ongoing: DHS Partial Shutdown — Day 30+

The two-week DHS continuing resolution expired on February 14 without a deal, triggering a second partial shutdown affecting the Transportation Security Administration, FEMA, the Coast Guard, and the Secret Service. As of March 16, the shutdown is ongoing. TSA workers missed their first full paycheck around March 14. The House passed a full-year DHS funding bill (H.R. 7744) on March 5, 221–209, but Senate Democrats have blocked it from reaching the 60-vote threshold required for floor consideration.

What makes the DHS standoff structurally significant is what it reveals about the relationship between spending power, lobbying influence, and national debt. DHS commands a budget of $65 billion under baseline estimates — and the Trump administration's FY2026 request proposed increasing that to over $107 billion, a 65% jump driven primarily by immigration enforcement priorities. When that funding is in dispute, it doesn't just affect federal workers: it ripples outward into a web of government contractors, local governments dependent on FEMA grants, airport operators, and border-adjacent businesses, all of whom have active lobbying presences in Washington.

Speaking of lobbying: this quarter's activity confirms that the passage of the One Big Beautiful Bill Act (OBBA) in 2025 dramatically reorganized the influence landscape. Industries that saw their fortunes written into or out of that reconciliation law — pharmaceuticals, defense contractors, education companies, clean energy firms — have been spending at record clips in an effort to shape both the regulatory implementation of OBBA and the appropriations bills that translate its priorities into dollars. The pharmaceutical industry outspent every other sector in 2025 by a wide margin, and there is no sign of that easing in 2026. Ballard Partners, a lobbying firm with deep ties to the current administration, continued to dominate the K Street rankings.

All of this unfolds against the most alarming debt backdrop in American history. As of March 4, 2026, the national debt stood at $38.86 trillion — up $2.64 trillion in a single year, or roughly $7.23 billion per day. Debt held by the public has crossed 101% of GDP, a threshold the country last breached during World War II. The Congressional Budget Office projects the FY2026 deficit alone at $1.9 trillion, with net interest payments consuming nearly 14 cents of every federal dollar spent. As the Committee for a Responsible Federal Budget recently warned: we are entering the next potential economic shock "more indebted than we have ever been before."

The fiscal recklessness here is not primarily the fault of discretionary spending, which the eleven enacted FY2026 bills actually held relatively flat at $1.639 trillion total — only $38.5 billion above FY2025 levels. The deeper problem is structural: mandatory spending on Social Security, Medicare, and Medicaid growing automatically year over year, compounded by soaring interest costs that now dwarf what we spent on defense just a decade ago. Congress can debate DHS immigration policy all it wants, but the most consequential budget decisions are the ones no one votes on each year.

§ II Follow the Money
Appropriations · National Debt · Lobbying

FY2026 Funding Appropriations

The eleven enacted appropriations bills total $1.639 trillion in net base discretionary spending for FY2026. This figure is deceptive on its surface: because the bills include more than 100 rescissions totaling $55.2 billion in paper cuts to unused budget authority, actual new spending authority is closer to $1.7 trillion. Defense received a significant boost, while the Departments of Education and the EPA saw reduced outlays — the former driven by lower Education Stabilization Fund disbursements, the latter reflecting the rollback of clean energy grants.

    The enacted bills represent a genuine compromise in what was a deeply contentious process. The Commerce-Justice-Science, Energy-Water, and Interior-Environment package (P.L. 119-74, signed January 28) actually cut spending from FY2025 levels by nearly $4 billion. The larger Defense/Labor-HHS/Education/State/Transport-HUD package (P.L. 119-75, signed February 3) added more but kept the overall trajectory well below what a straight continuing resolution would have cost taxpayers. The missing piece — DHS, with its $65–107 billion footprint — remains in limbo.

    The CBO's latest projections forecast the FY2026 deficit at $1.9 trillion — 5.8% of GDP. This is not a one-time shock. Net interest payments alone are projected to consume 13.85% of all federal outlays in FY2026, rising to 14.52% by FY2028. By 2036, debt held by the public is on pace to reach 120% of GDP, surpassing even the post–World War II peak of 106%. The Committee for a Responsible Federal Budget notes that when prior crisis moments — the dot-com crash, 2008, COVID — struck, the U.S. had debt-to-GDP ratios of 34%, 35%, and 79% respectively. Today the ratio is 101%. The fiscal buffer is gone.

    Revenues in the first two months of FY2026 were actually $112 billion (18%) higher than the same period last year, driven by strong individual income tax collections and a $48 billion (287%) surge in customs duties from expanded tariffs. But that revenue growth has been swamped by mandatory spending increases in Social Security (+$10B), Medicare/Medicaid (+$10B), and net interest on the debt (+$9B) — automatic obligations no appropriations bill can touch.

    Lobbying: The Influence Economy

    Formal Q1 2026 lobbying disclosure filings are not yet published (reports are due mid-April, covering January through March activity). However, patterns established in 2025 provide a clear picture of where influence money is flowing and why. Lobbying spending surged dramatically in 2025 coinciding with the One Big Beautiful Bill Act, setting records across nearly every major sector. 2026 is shaping up as the implementation year — lobbying is now focused on how OBBA provisions are administered and on shaping the annual appropriations that will fund (or defund) them.

    The most important lobbying story of Q1 2026 is one that connects directly to the DHS standoff: private immigration detention contractors — including GEO Group and CoreCivic — have been among the most active spenders in the sector, seeking to lock in long-term federal contracts funded through both the OBBA's mandatory appropriations and whatever DHS spending bill eventually passes. On the opposite side of the ledger, renewable energy and environmental groups — watching EPA budget cuts and IRA rescissions take effect — have dramatically increased their lobbying presence, seeking to protect whatever green infrastructure dollars remain from congressional and regulatory rollback.

    Ballard Partners, whose leadership includes former Trump administration officials, remained the premier lobbying firm in terms of billings and access during this period, continuing a dominance that began the moment the administration took office in January 2025. The revolving door between K Street and the executive branch has rarely turned faster.

    § III Forecast
    Major Upcoming Bills & Political Hurdles · Q2 and Beyond

    Looking ahead to Q2 2026 and the remainder of the legislative year, five major items are shaping up as the defining battles of this Congress. Each carries significant fiscal implications, and each is being actively shaped by the lobbying activity documented above.

    DHS Appropriations Act, FY2026 (H.R. 7744)

    Priority: Critical

    The most immediate and urgent piece of unfinished business. The House has passed this bill twice. Senate Democrats continue to block it pending immigration enforcement reforms. TSA workers are beginning to miss full paychecks as of mid-March, raising the prospect of security line disruptions at airports nationwide. A resolution — whether through a negotiated reform package or a clean CR — is expected before April, but nothing is certain. The longer the shutdown persists, the greater the pressure on both parties as the spring travel season arrives and the 2026 midterms draw closer.

    National Defense Authorization Act, FY2026 (H.R. 3838 / S. 2296)

    Priority: High

    The annual must-pass defense authorization bill is in conference between House and Senate versions. Both drafts prioritize domestic supply chain resiliency, rare earth and critical minerals, advanced AI and autonomous weapons systems, and the "Golden Dome" missile defense initiative. Counter-drone authority for major events (the 2026 FIFA World Cup, LA 2028 Olympics) is a bipartisan priority. Preemption of state AI regulations — inserted in the House version, stripped in the Senate — remains a live fault line. A final vote is expected before summer recess.

    FY2027 Budget Resolution & Potential Reconciliation Bill

    Priority: Elevated

    With the midterm elections in November 2026, Republican leadership is eyeing a second reconciliation bill to further lock in fiscal priorities before potentially losing House majority. The vehicle would likely include additional defense technology investments, border security spending, and extensions of tax provisions from OBBA. The CBO has already warned that OBBA increased projected 10-year deficits by $4.7 trillion. A follow-on reconciliation measure without offsets could add substantially to that figure. Expect fierce debate over the debt ceiling, which will need to be addressed before fiscal year 2027 begins.

    NASA Reauthorization

    Priority: Moderate

    NASA's authorization is overdue for a comprehensive update, particularly around the Artemis lunar program's revised scope and timeline. Both NDAA drafts include Space Force and National Security Space Launch provisions, and any NASA bill will need to navigate the politically charged terrain of Artemis funding, commercial space partnerships, and the FAA's regulatory modernization — all of which carry significant contractor lobbying interests.

    SBIR / STTR Reauthorization

    Priority: Monitoring

    The Small Business Innovation Research and Small Business Technology Transfer programs — which direct a portion of federal R&D spending to small businesses — face reauthorization. These programs are often included in the NDAA as a vehicle. If they slip past the NDAA deadline, a standalone bill becomes necessary. Stakes are modest fiscally but matter significantly to the startup and academic research communities.

    Political Hurdles to Watch

    ⚠ The 60-Vote Senate Threshold

    The DHS shutdown has thrown into stark relief how the Senate filibuster — requiring 60 votes to advance most legislation — continues to shape (and block) major legislation. Republicans hold 53 seats and cannot move DHS funding without some Democratic cooperation. This dynamic will define Q2 legislative strategy across every major bill.

    ⚠ The Debt Ceiling

    The CBO projects debt will reach $39 trillion as early as late March 2026 and continue climbing toward $40 trillion by late 2026. The debt ceiling — last raised by the OBBA — will need to be addressed again, likely during FY2027 budget negotiations. Fiscal hawks in both parties have signaled that a clean debt ceiling raise will be difficult to pass without accompanying spending reforms, setting up a potential crisis moment in late Q3 or Q4 2026.

    ⚠ Midterm Positioning

    With the November 2026 midterms just eight months away, every major vote is now viewed through an electoral lens. House Republicans, holding a razor-thin majority, face internal pressures from fiscal conservatives who oppose any spending increases and from moderates who fear being tagged with shutdown politics. Democrats, out of power but wielding the Senate filibuster, must weigh the political cost of sustaining a DHS shutdown against the leverage it provides on immigration reform. The result is a Congress where the incentive to govern is frequently overwhelmed by the incentive to campaign.

    ⚠ AI Regulation Preemption

    A sleeper issue that could become a major fight: the House NDAA includes language preempting state-level AI regulations, effectively handing regulatory authority to the federal government. At least 16 states have passed or are considering their own AI rules. Tech industry lobbyists support federal preemption; civil society and several state governors oppose it. If this provision survives conference and reaches a floor vote, it will be one of the most consequential tech-policy decisions in years — regardless of which direction it goes.
